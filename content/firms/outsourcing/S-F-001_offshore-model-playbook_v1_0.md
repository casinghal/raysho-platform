# Offshore Accounting for CPA Firms: Why Choosing the Model Matters More Than Choosing the Country

Most CPA firms that attempt to build an offshore delivery wing fail for the same reason. They choose the country first. Philippines. India. Colombia. Then they go looking for a vendor, a model, a pricing structure that fits the country they already picked.

The sequence is wrong. The country is a secondary decision. The model determines cost curve, control, legal exposure, and exit flexibility. The country follows from the model, not the other way around.

Model first. Country second. Vendor third.

## 1. The four offshore models you can actually run

Before any conversation about Manila versus Bangalore versus Mumbai happens, the firm has to know which of these four structures it is building toward. Each has a different cost curve, a different control surface, and a different legal shape.

**Captive.** You stand up your own legal entity offshore. You hire the team directly. Payroll runs through your entity. You own the IP, the contracts, and the staff relationships. Maximum control. Maximum upfront cost. Break-even around 15 to 25 FTE depending on country.

**Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT).** A local partner sets up and runs the team for the first 18 to 36 months. At a predefined trigger you take ownership. The entity, staff, and contracts move to you. Useful when you want captive eventually but do not want to stand up a country office cold. Cost is higher than managed services for the first two years, lower in years three through five.

**Managed services.** A vendor runs the team. You pay per FTE, per deliverable, or per retainer. The vendor owns the staff relationships, compliance, and facilities. You own the client relationship and the work quality. Fastest stand-up. Lowest upfront cost. Weakest moat. Viable for 1 to 10 FTE of capacity. Above that, the vendor's margin starts to look like your captive's operating cost.

**Staff augmentation.** A vendor places individual offshore staff under your direction. They sit on your tools, attend your standups, follow your SOPs. Payroll runs through the vendor. Closest to captive in feel, without the entity. Cost sits between managed services and captive. Legal exposure is higher than managed services because you exert day-to-day control without being the employer on paper.

Four models. Four cost curves. Four risk profiles. Picking the wrong one is how firms burn 12 months and six figures before realising the problem was never the country.

## 2. What the model choice is actually deciding

The four models look like pricing structures. They are not. They are decisions about four separate questions, and the firm that gets the model right is the firm that has answered all four before the country conversation starts.

**How much control do you need over daily work quality?** Captive and staff augmentation give you direct control. Managed services gives you SLA-based control, which is not the same thing. If your work is highly repetitive and heavily documented, SLA control is enough. If your work involves judgment calls a junior bookkeeper cannot make without mentoring, you need daily direction. That points to captive or staff augmentation.

**What is your exit cost if this does not work?** Managed services has the cleanest exit, typically a 30 to 90 day notice. Captive has the highest. You are winding down a legal entity, settling staff contracts, dealing with country-specific severance rules. BOT is the hardest to exit mid-way because the trigger structure assumes you were going to take ownership.

**How fast do you need capacity?** Managed services: weeks. Staff augmentation: weeks to months. BOT: 3 to 6 months. Captive: 6 to 12 months. The firm that tells you it can stand up a captive in 90 days is either lying or leaving compliance for later.

**What is the break-even FTE count?** This is the number everyone skips. It is also the number that settles the argument.

Here is the math most vendor pitches do not show you. A Philippines managed services contract typically bills $2,200 to $3,000 per FTE per month, all in. A Philippines captive costs roughly $1,100 to $1,400 per FTE per month steady-state once the entity is running, but you carry $60,000 to $90,000 of setup cost and six to nine months of run-rate burn before the first seat is productive. Run the math on a spreadsheet and the crossover happens between 8 and 12 FTE, depending on the city and the benefits loading.

Below the crossover, managed services wins on cost and on speed. Above it, captive wins on cost and on control. Staff augmentation sits in a separate column because the decision there is less about the FTE count and more about whether you can live with directing staff you do not formally employ.

The firm that does this math before the country conversation is the firm that does not end up 14 months in, discovering it built the wrong structure for the capacity it actually needed.

## 3. Country selection

Now the country conversation earns a seat.

For US CPA firms, 80 percent of viable build-outs land in two markets: the Philippines and India. The rest of the world shows up as edge cases or sub-10-FTE boutique plays. The three-tier framing that follows is for the partner who wants to know why, not just what.

### Tier 1: Philippines and India

**Philippines.** English fluency at senior bookkeeping level is exceptional. Cultural alignment with US work norms is the strongest of any offshore market, which shows up as deadline orientation, client service tone, and direct escalation behaviour. All-in cost: $1,100 to $1,400 per FTE per month captive, or $2,200 to $2,800 managed services. Accounting labor pool is deep but concentrated in Manila and Cebu. Expect a 10 to 15 percent premium over Indian rates for equivalent seniority. Timezone: GMT+8. For US Eastern, that is a 12 to 13 hour gap, which makes real-time collaboration hard but end-of-day handoff clean.

**India.** Larger accounting labor pool. ICAI alone has 3.4 million members and 40,000 new Chartered Accountants qualifying every year. Cost floor is lower: $900 to $1,200 per FTE per month captive, $1,800 to $2,500 managed. English fluency is high at the mid and senior level but more variable at junior seats. Explicit SOPs carry a bigger share of the communication load than in the Philippines. Cultural calibration takes longer in the first 90 days and pays off for the next decade. Timezone: GMT+5:30. For US firms, a 9:30 to 10:30 hour gap. A real overlap window exists if the Indian team starts at 7am local. That gives you 9:00 to 10:30am ET of live collaboration.

### Tier 2: Colombia, South Africa, Kenya

**Colombia.** Timezone match with US Eastern is the draw. English second, Spanish first. Cost sits between Manila and Bangalore. Pool for US GAAP bookkeeping is still small.

**South Africa.** English-native. Cost similar to Philippines. Smaller accounting labor pool. Strong legal and corporate governance culture. Load-shedding (scheduled power outages) is a real operational risk that needs contingency.

**Kenya.** English-native. Most affordable of any Tier 1 or Tier 2 market. Growing CPA-equivalent pool. Infrastructure and political risk factors need hard checks before anything beyond a 3 to 5 FTE pilot.

### Tier 3

Vietnam, Egypt, Mexico, Poland (for EU firms). Skip unless a specific driver exists. Existing client base, founder connection, or a tax treaty advantage. The accounting labor pool is too thin to scale past 5 to 10 FTE without hitting quality walls.

### Country decision rules

For managed services or staff augmentation, 1 to 10 FTE: Philippines or India. Pick based on your partner's personal comfort with one versus the other. Either works at that scale.

For captive or BOT, 10-plus FTE: India if cost is the binding constraint, Philippines if cultural calibration is the binding constraint. Both decisions are defensible.

For any structure, urgent ramp (under 60 days): Philippines managed services. India's hiring cycle runs longer and the first productive seat typically lands 4 to 6 weeks later than Manila.

## 4. The 90-day stand-up

Day-by-day plans are a fantasy. Week-by-week is what actually works. Specifics shift by model.

### Weeks 1 to 2: Decision lock and paperwork

Confirm model and country. Kick off entity formation if captive. This is the longest-lead item and blocks everything downstream. Issue vendor RFP if managed or staff augmentation. Draft data-handling protocols, engagement letter addenda for offshore work, and the client-communication script for partners to use when clients ask.

### Weeks 3 to 4: Recruitment and legal

Captive: file entity paperwork, engage a local employer-of-record for the interim, begin senior hire interviews. BOT: sign the BOT agreement with your local partner, and they begin hiring against your spec. Managed services: sign MSA, finalise SOW, vendor begins assigning staff. Staff augmentation: vendor delivers shortlist, your side runs final interviews.

Parallel work for all models: data access architecture decisions land this week. VPN, VDI, or cloud-direct. Document data-minimisation rules. What leaves your tenant, what does not.

### Weeks 5 to 6: SOP build and first-team assembly

First three to five SOPs go from tribal knowledge into written form. Bank reconciliation. Month-end close. Client communication protocol. Record video walkthroughs, not just text. The offshore team watches, tries, asks questions, and you iterate the SOP based on the questions.

Captive and BOT: first hires start. Training budget locked. Typically 2 to 4 weeks of non-billable ramp per seat.

### Weeks 7 to 8: Pilot client

Choose one client for the offshore pilot. Ideally an existing client with whom you have a strong relationship and whose work is representative but not unusually complex. Run the work twice: offshore team produces, onshore reviewer checks every number for the first two monthly closes.

This is where half the failures happen. The pilot client gets treated as a test case, quality slips, the client notices, and the firm concludes offshoring does not work. The pilot must be run with better quality than your internal default, not lower, because you are comparing to your own best work and not to your own average.

### Weeks 9 to 12: Quality gate and scale decision

By end of week 12, the pilot client's books should meet your firm's normal quality standard with review time that is falling week over week. If review time is still at 100 percent after week 12, the pilot has not worked. Diagnose the cause. SOP gap, skill gap, or tooling gap. Fix it before adding a second client.

Add client two at week 10 to 12 only if the pilot is clearly stable.

## 5. Five legal and tax gotchas

Each of these has killed at least one offshore build that I have seen up close.

**One: US tax residency for offshore staff.** If your captive entity has no US nexus and your offshore staff are non-US persons working outside the US, there is usually no US tax exposure on them. The trap sits elsewhere. When you fly an offshore team member to the US for a training sprint or a client meeting, you can create unintended nexus depending on the state, the length of stay, and the nature of the work performed. Get a US tax opinion before any in-person visit beyond tourist-activity length.

**Two: Data export restrictions.** India, the Philippines, the EU, and California all have separate data-handling regimes. India's DPDPA (2023), the Philippines' Data Privacy Act, GDPR for EU client data, and CCPA for California client data can all apply to the same workflow. A single client with operations in multiple jurisdictions can trigger three or four overlapping regimes. Do not assume your managed services vendor has handled this. Ask for the specific contractual clauses and the data-flow map before signing.

**Three: State CPA licensure implications.** Most state boards treat offshore work as work performed by staff under the supervision of a licensed CPA, which is allowed. Some states, and the AICPA's own position as it evolves, are narrowing the range of who can sign, review, and attest. Offshore staff generally cannot sign engagement letters or review audits. Confirm the current position with your state board before your first audit-season offshore deployment.

**Four: SOC 2 expectations and what that really costs.** If you are serving mid-market clients or any client with investor reporting obligations, SOC 2 Type II is increasingly non-negotiable for you or your vendor. Budget $40,000 to $80,000 for the first SOC 2 Type II audit and ongoing $25,000 to $40,000 per year for maintenance. That covers the auditor, the control evidence, and the tooling. Managed services vendors often carry SOC 2 themselves. Verify the certification letter and the scope before you sign. SOC 2 with a carve-out for the work your firm actually does is worth less than it looks.

**Five: Engagement letter language.** Your existing engagement letter likely does not cover offshore work explicitly. At minimum you need a disclosure that some work may be performed by offshore staff under supervision, a data-handling clause matching the vendor's and your own posture, and a confidentiality clause that survives both engagement termination and vendor change. If a client objects, that is useful information. Some clients cannot allow offshore work at all because of their own downstream compliance obligations. Better to find that out in the engagement-letter stage than after the first month's deliverable.

## 6. The operator reference

Most guides at this point reach for a named case study. I am not going to, for a specific reason.

I have run F&A delivery for clients in the US, Canada, UK, and Australia for three years. Before that, 15 years in senior F&A operations at Accenture, Genpact, R1 RCM, where I ran or supported offshore stand-ups across India and the Philippines at scale. The patterns in this playbook are drawn from that work and from live delivery. I am not citing client names because client confidentiality is the first test of whether a delivery operator deserves to run your books.

In place of a named case, two operational patterns that repeat often enough to be worth naming.

**Pattern one: the first-close stall.** Month one of a new offshore engagement almost always takes longer than projected. Not because the offshore team is slow, but because the SOP drafted in weeks 5 to 6 turns out to be incomplete when it meets real client data in weeks 7 to 8. The fix is not more training. It is faster SOP iteration during the pilot. Publish a revised SOP within 48 hours of each gap discovered, and treat SOP version count as a positive leading indicator for the first 90 days.

**Pattern two: the reviewer bottleneck.** Most offshore builds discover in months 3 to 5 that the bottleneck is not offshore capacity but onshore review capacity. Every offshore deliverable still needs a US-based reviewer until the quality gate passes. If you do not plan for that review capacity, the offshore team produces faster than you can absorb. Allocate onshore reviewer time at 20 to 30 percent of offshore capacity for the first six months, tapering to 10 to 15 percent steady-state.

---

Most CPA firms that try to offshore and fail get the country right and the model wrong. They find a vendor that fits the wrong model, and they discover 14 months in that the structure will not hold. The sequence in this playbook is model, then country, then vendor. That is the one I wish I had found in writing the first time I stood one of these up.

Build the structure that matches the capacity you actually need. Pick the country that supports that structure. Pick the vendor last, and only if managed services or staff augmentation made the cut earlier. If the numbers say captive, no vendor needs to be in the conversation at all.
