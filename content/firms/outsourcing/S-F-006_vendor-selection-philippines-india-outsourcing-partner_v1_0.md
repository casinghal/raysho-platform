# Vendor Selection for CPA Firm Outsourcing: The Three Conversations That Reveal Whether a Philippines or India Partner Can Deliver

A CPA firm partner has shortlisted four outsourcing vendors after two weeks of demos. All four decks show the same photographs of clean offices, the same claim about SOC 2 compliance, the same logo wall of "current clients," and roughly the same price. At the end of the fourth demo the partner realises the selection has not actually narrowed. Every vendor looks equally credible because every vendor's sales process is built to look credible. The decision is now being made on impression, hourly rate, and which sales lead has been most persistent.

That is how most external-vendor selections go, and that is why the two-year failure rate for CPA firm outsourcing partnerships runs as high as it does. The vendor decks are not the signal. The sales conversations are not the signal. The LinkedIn case studies are not the signal. The signal lives in three specific conversations that the vendor sales team does not run, and will not always volunteer, but a disciplined partner can insist on.

S-F-001 settled the model question. If the chosen model is external vendor (managed services or staff augmentation), this playbook runs after the decision has been made and before any contract is signed. It scripts the three conversations, lists the twelve red flags that surface in them, names the five contract clauses that do the real risk work, and provides a scorecard template that forces honest comparison across vendors.

What follows is in five parts. Section 1 names why sales-led selection fails. Section 2 frames the three-conversation architecture. Sections 3 through 5 script each conversation with specific questions (forty across the three, grouped by purpose). Section 6 catalogues twelve red flags with what they sound like in the conversation. Section 7 lists the five contract clauses that carry the real risk transfer. Section 8 walks the weighted scorecard template. Section 9 closes with three antipatterns.

## 1. Why sales-led selection fails

The vendor sales process is engineered to pass selection. That sentence is not a criticism of sales teams; it is a description of what the function exists to do. A competent sales team will produce a deck that looks like every other competent sales team's deck. A competent sales team will line up reference clients who are willing to give a warm call. A competent sales team will send a proposal at the price point the partner has signalled. None of this surfaces the delivery reality.

Three specific failure patterns come from accepting the sales process as the selection process.

**The demo trap.** The vendor's senior delivery lead runs the demo, answers all the technical questions well, and talks fluently about close calendars, QBO workflows, and SLA frameworks. At contract signing, the delivery lead the client actually gets is three rungs lower, trained in the vendor's process, competent enough, but without the judgement the demo lead showed. The demo lead was not lying about capability. The demo lead was the vendor's best case, not the average case.

**The case-study illusion.** The vendor's case studies describe large engagements with names the partner recognises. Two things about those case studies. First, the named clients are not always the vendor's typical client; they are their largest, oldest, and most forgiving. Second, the case studies are written by marketing, not by delivery, and they describe outcomes without the transition work, the first-year fixes, or the client-side effort that made the outcomes possible. Case studies are not the signal.

**The price-anchoring play.** The vendor prices close enough to the partner's mental budget that price stops being a filter. The partner then moves to "cultural fit" as the tiebreaker, which is a non-measurable criterion that reduces to "who I liked on the call." Vendors who have been through enough partner selections know this pattern and execute it deliberately. The partner thinks they chose on instinct. The vendor knows the instinct was engineered.

The three conversations below exist to move the decision off sales ground and onto delivery ground, client ground, and compliance ground. They cannot be optional. A vendor that will not facilitate them has told the partner something important about how the relationship will run.

## 2. The three conversations: architecture

Each of the three conversations has a different purpose, a different counterparty, and a different set of questions. Running one well and skipping the others produces a biased picture. All three run before the scorecard is scored.

**Conversation 1 is with the delivery manager** who will lead the account. Not the sales engineer. Not the senior delivery lead on the pitch call who then hands off. The specific named person whose name will appear on the statement of work as the delivery manager. Purpose: verify competence, process discipline, and communication style at the layer that will own the work day to day.

**Conversation 2 is with a current client** the partner has located independently. Not a client on the vendor's reference list. The vendor-supplied list is pre-screened for warmth. The partner finds a current or recent client through their own network, a LinkedIn search on "using [vendor name] for outsourced accounting," an industry association membership list, or a direct approach through a professional body. Purpose: hear the unedited delivery experience, including what broke and how it was handled.

**Conversation 3 is with the vendor's compliance officer** or whoever owns SOC 2, DPDPA or GDPR response, data-export controls, and sub-processor management. Not the sales lead repeating compliance claims. Purpose: establish whether the compliance posture is documented at depth or marketing-thin.

Each conversation is scheduled as its own meeting, thirty to forty-five minutes. No conversation is held on a sales call. The vendor is told at the outset that all three are a precondition of the scorecard being completed, and that a vendor who cannot or will not facilitate any one of the three is removed from the shortlist at that point.

## 3. Conversation 1: the delivery manager

Thirteen questions. The goal is to surface how this specific person thinks about delivery, not to audit their CV. CV audits run separately.

### Experience and fit

1. Which three accounts on your current book look most like our firm in size, client mix, and complexity? Walk me through one of them from onboarding to current state.

2. On that account, what was the hardest thing about the first ninety days, and what did you do about it?

3. Which part of the onboarding tends to go slower than clients expect, and why?

### Process and discipline

4. Describe your month-end close calendar for a typical client. Who owns which day, and what is the escalation if a day slips?

5. Walk me through how you handle a bank feed that breaks on the third day of close week.

6. What does your quality-control process look like? Who reviews whose work, and what percentage of closes get reviewed at what depth?

7. How do you document exceptions and one-off client decisions so the next reviewer sees them?

### Communication and governance

8. What is your standing cadence with a client partner? What is the agenda?

9. When a close is going to miss the target date, when and how do you communicate that, and to whom?

10. What is your approach to client data requests that arrive outside the monthly cycle? Describe the last ad-hoc request you handled and the turnaround.

### Judgement

11. Describe a close where the numbers told you one story and the client's commentary told you another. How did you resolve it?

12. What is a mistake your team has made in the last six months that you would want me to know about, and what did you put in place after it?

13. If the engagement was offered to you today, what would you want in the statement of work that is not in the RFP?

### How to read the answers

Specificity is the signal. Answers that sound like case studies, use vendor-branded methodology names, or retreat to process abstractions are warnings. Answers that name specific client types, specific incidents, specific remedies, and specific learnings are what a competent delivery manager sounds like when they trust the conversation. Question 12 in particular separates candid operators from trained spokespeople. A delivery manager who cannot name a recent mistake has either been coached to deflect or is not close enough to the work to know about one.

## 4. Conversation 2: the independently located current client

Fourteen questions. This conversation is only valuable if the client has been found independently of the vendor's reference list. The vendor's list is warm. The purpose of this conversation is to hear the cold version.

### Finding the client

Before the questions, the finding method. LinkedIn search for "outsourced [vendor name]" or "[vendor name] accounting" typically surfaces current or former clients. Industry-association member directories in the partner's space often list firms that use offshore delivery. A direct approach through a professional body (state CPA society, ICAEW, CPA Canada, CA ANZ) asking "who in your network uses [vendor name] and would be willing to speak to someone evaluating them" produces named referrals at least half the time. Cold approaches work. The partner states purpose, states a fifteen-minute time request, and offers reciprocity if possible.

### Onboarding experience

1. How long did the onboarding actually take against the vendor's quoted timeline?

2. What was the single biggest gap between what you expected and what showed up in the first ninety days?

3. What would you do differently about the onboarding now, if you were starting again?

### Ongoing delivery

4. How accurate is the monthly close against the committed calendar? Over the last year, how many closes slipped, and by how much?

5. How much of your time, as the client partner, actually goes into the relationship each month?

6. When the work is wrong, how is it wrong? What are the typical errors you find?

7. Who on the vendor side gets your call when something is urgent? How quickly do they respond?

### Communication and escalation

8. Has a delivery manager changed on you? How was that handled?

9. Describe the worst incident in the relationship so far and how it was resolved.

10. Do you feel the vendor hides problems from you, or surfaces them?

### Value and pricing

11. How has pricing moved since you signed? What triggers repricing conversations?

12. What is the vendor billing you for that you do not think you are getting value from?

### Counterfactual

13. If you were shopping for a vendor today, would you pick this one again? Why or why not?

14. If there is one thing you would warn me about before I sign, what is it?

### How to read the answers

Length and texture. Clients willing to speak freely give longer, more textured answers. Clients on a warm-reference list tend to give shorter, positioned answers. Question 14 is the direct hand. A client who has nothing to warn about has either been screened, has been a client for less than six months, or is being polite. Press once for specificity; if nothing surfaces, that itself is information.

## 5. Conversation 3: the compliance officer

Thirteen questions. The compliance officer conversation exists to distinguish documented compliance from marketing-thin compliance. A vendor claiming SOC 2 can mean the Type 1 report from eighteen months ago exists, or it can mean an annual Type 2 report is in the system, covering the exact delivery environment that will handle the partner's client data. The questions below surface which.

### SOC 2 or equivalent

1. Which SOC 2 (or ISO 27001) report covers the environment my client data will sit in? What is the scope and the period? May I see the latest report under NDA?

2. What were the exceptions in the most recent report, and what remediation was done?

3. What is the re-testing cycle? When is the next report due?

### Data handling

4. Where physically does my client data reside? Which country, which cloud region, which data centre?

5. What are your data-export restrictions on your delivery staff? Describe the controls that prevent a delivery team member from moving a client file off your environment.

6. Describe your sub-processor list. Which third parties get access to client data, for what purpose, and under what legal basis?

7. How do you handle a client deletion request? Walk me through the sequence from request to confirmation.

### Jurisdictional and regulatory

8. What is your DPDPA (India) or Data Privacy Act (Philippines) posture? How does it interact with my regulatory environment (state CPA rules, GDPR, CCPA)?

9. Does any part of my client data fall under US state CPA licensure restrictions (non-attest work performed by non-licensed preparers in a non-US jurisdiction)? How do you handle the regulatory boundary?

10. What is your breach notification policy? What is the notification timeline and who gets told?

### Governance

11. How often do you test your incident response plan, and when was the last test?

12. What is the background-check standard for the staff who will touch my client data?

13. If I ask for an annual third-party audit of the environment my data sits in, is that available? At what cost?

### How to read the answers

Depth and document presence. A compliance officer who can produce the SOC 2 report under NDA during the call, name specific exceptions, walk the sub-processor list, and speak specifically to the jurisdictional boundary is running a real compliance function. A compliance officer who speaks in certifications, defers specifics to the sales team, or refuses to produce documentation under NDA is running a marketing function with a compliance title. The distinction matters because the compliance function is what the partner relies on during an actual incident.

## 6. Red-flag catalogue: twelve specific signals

These are the twelve signals that have appeared most often in CPA firm outsourcing partnerships that failed within two years. The red flag is the observable phenomenon in the selection conversation. The failure mode is what the red flag predicts.

**Red flag 1: the demo lead disappears after contract.** Partner is assured the demo lead will stay on the account. The statement of work names someone else. Failure mode: the vendor's best person is a sales asset, not a delivery asset.

**Red flag 2: reference clients are all more than two years old.** Partner cannot find a client who signed in the last twelve months. Failure mode: the vendor is losing new clients faster than keeping old ones, and the partner is being asked to become the new logo.

**Red flag 3: no named delivery manager before signature.** The vendor will only assign the delivery manager after the contract is signed. Failure mode: the partner cannot verify the actual delivery resource until it is too late to change the decision.

**Red flag 4: SOC 2 scope does not include the delivery environment.** The SOC 2 report exists but covers a production infrastructure different from where client data will actually be processed. Failure mode: the compliance claim is technically true and operationally meaningless.

**Red flag 5: sub-processor list undisclosed.** The vendor will not share the list of third parties who touch client data. Failure mode: the partner cannot assess downstream risk or do their own due diligence on any of those parties.

**Red flag 6: no client termination history discussed.** The vendor cannot or will not describe a client who left and why. Failure mode: the vendor is either concealing the pattern or is not learning from it. Either case predicts repetition.

**Red flag 7: vague answers on data export controls.** Compliance officer cannot specifically describe how delivery staff are prevented from moving client data off the vendor environment. Failure mode: the controls probably do not exist at the claimed level.

**Red flag 8: pricing bundled to prevent comparison.** Vendor refuses to separate the cost of the delivery team from the cost of technology, onboarding, and reporting. Failure mode: the partner cannot see where the value actually is and cannot renegotiate specific line items later.

**Red flag 9: exclusivity or minimum volume commitments in the first year.** Vendor insists on a minimum client count or minimum monthly spend during the first twelve months. Failure mode: the partner is locked in before delivery quality is proven.

**Red flag 10: no written escalation process.** The vendor describes escalation as "call me" rather than a documented ladder with response-time commitments. Failure mode: escalation becomes an interpersonal negotiation every time, not a procedural one.

**Red flag 11: the reference client is on the vendor's advisory board.** The reference client has a commercial or advisory relationship with the vendor. Failure mode: the reference is a fiduciary of the vendor and cannot speak freely about delivery issues.

**Red flag 12: the contract has no exit clause.** There is no defined off-boarding procedure, data-return clause, or cost of exit. Failure mode: leaving the vendor becomes a legal and operational ordeal that the partner does not want to pay for, which means staying becomes the default regardless of performance.

## 7. The five contract clauses that carry the real risk

Five clauses do most of the risk work. Negotiate them at contract. Do not accept standard vendor terms on these five without review. Legal counsel drafts the clauses; this section explains what the partner asks counsel to insist on.

**Clause 1: SLA enforcement.** The SLA itself is covered in S-F-003. The contract clause specifies what happens when the SLA is breached. Credit-back of fees, a defined escalation path, and after repeated breaches, a termination right without cause. The specific numbers (credit percentage, breach threshold, termination trigger) are the negotiation. The principle is that SLA breach has a defined consequence, not a conversation.

**Clause 2: data ownership and return.** The client data the partner's firm sends to the vendor is, and remains, the firm's data and (by extension) the firm's client's data. The contract says so explicitly. The clause also specifies the format in which data will be returned on termination, the timeframe, and the verification method (checksums, file counts, completeness certificate). No clause on this is the default position that data belongs to whoever holds it physically, which is not where the partner wants to be.

**Clause 3: exit clause.** A defined off-boarding procedure: notice period (90 days is typical), data return timeline, transition cooperation (including handover calls with successor), and cost. The clause names what the vendor will charge, if anything, for the exit cooperation. An exit priced at zero or a stated cap is achievable with most vendors at contract and is much harder to add later.

**Clause 4: liability cap.** The vendor's liability is capped at a multiple of fees paid over the preceding twelve months. Typical multiples are 1x to 2x. The higher end is negotiable for larger engagements. The principle is that a cap exists and is proportional to the fees. The common vendor default (cap equal to last month's fees, or a nominal sum) is inadequate for any material engagement.

**Clause 5: SOC 2 maintenance and audit right.** The vendor commits to maintaining SOC 2 (or ISO 27001) coverage of the delivery environment for the term of the contract, with annual renewal. The clause includes the partner's right to request a copy of each report under NDA. For larger engagements, an audit right clause allows the partner or a nominated third party to conduct an independent audit at the partner's cost. The audit right is often resisted by smaller vendors; the response is to negotiate it as a high-cost-trigger right rather than drop it entirely.

Five clauses. Each one closes a specific failure mode that Section 6 flagged. Without them, the scorecard score is meaningless because the partner is bearing the risk the score is supposed to reflect.

## 8. The weighted scorecard

The scorecard forces honest comparison across vendors whose surface pitches all look similar. Six categories, weighted, scored from 1 (poor) to 5 (strong) based on the evidence from the three conversations. A vendor below a floor score of 3.5 on weighted total is not shortlisted. A vendor who scores strong on every category except one severe red flag is not shortlisted either; severe red flags (flags 1, 3, 4, 5, 12 from Section 6) are knockout criteria regardless of weighted total.

### Scorecard structure

| Category | Weight | Scored from |
|---|---|---|
| Delivery manager quality | 25% | Conversation 1, question-by-question depth and specificity |
| Client-reported delivery | 20% | Conversation 2, length and texture of answers, question 14 response |
| Compliance and data handling | 20% | Conversation 3, document production during call, scope of SOC 2 |
| Contract terms | 15% | Willingness to accept the five clauses in Section 7 |
| Price realism | 10% | Price relative to scope without bundling tricks |
| Cultural fit and communication style | 10% | Aggregated across the three conversations |

Six categories. Total weight 100%. The weighting tilts toward the delivery manager and the client because those are the two hardest categories to recover from if wrong. The downloadable companion xlsx (`Raysho_Vendor_Selection_Scorecard_v1_0.xlsx`) runs the arithmetic, applies the knockout criteria, and produces a side-by-side for three or four shortlisted vendors.

Scoring discipline matters. The partner completes the scorecard alone, without the sales team present and without the shortlisted vendors present. Each score has a one-sentence justification referencing a specific moment in the three conversations. A score without a justification is a gut call and is marked as such. Final review is the partner reading the justifications together with whoever else in the firm will carry the delivery relationship (senior associate, controller, or operations lead).

## 9. Three antipatterns

**Antipattern 1: skipping conversation 3.** Partner runs conversations 1 and 2, decides the vendor looks strong enough, and defers the compliance conversation until contract negotiation. At contract, the compliance gaps surface, and the partner has already emotionally committed to this vendor. The gaps get negotiated away under time pressure or get accepted as "good enough." The compliance conversation has to run at selection stage, before the partner has any attachment to the outcome.

**Antipattern 2: accepting the vendor's reference list as the client conversation.** Partner speaks to two vendor-supplied references, hears warm reviews, and skips the independent-client step. The warm reviews are warm because the vendor has curated the list. The independent client is the one who gives the unedited delivery experience. Curated references are a check on vendor claims; they are not a substitute for a cold conversation.

**Antipattern 3: scoring before the three conversations complete.** Partner starts filling in the scorecard after conversation 1, adjusts after conversation 2, and is essentially defending an existing score by the time conversation 3 runs. The conversations have to complete before the scorecard opens. Anchoring bias is the single largest reason selection scorecards come out close to whatever the partner already leaned toward, and the anchor is set by whichever conversation is scored first.

---

Vendor selection is a disciplined decision that survives the sales process by moving onto ground the sales process does not cover. The three conversations put the decision on delivery ground, client ground, and compliance ground. The twelve red flags name the specific signals that predict failure. The five contract clauses close the specific risks the red flags expose. The scorecard forces the comparison to be explicit and justified.

A vendor who will facilitate all three conversations, produces documentation under NDA during the third, accepts the five clauses at contract, and scores above 3.5 on the weighted scorecard without triggering a knockout is a vendor the partner can sign with confidence. A vendor who cannot or will not meet those bars has told the partner everything that is needed. Walk. The cost of walking at selection is a week of re-work. The cost of walking after contract is measured in lost clients, compliance exposure, and the partner time that comes out of every remediation cycle for the next two years.

S-F-001 set the model. S-F-002, S-F-003, S-F-004, and S-F-005 operate whatever model has been chosen. This piece ensures that if the model is external vendor, the vendor earned the work before they got it.
